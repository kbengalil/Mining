Don Durant: Why to Buy Gold and Silver Mining Stocks

Gold and silver mining stocks are very unusual investments — more like speculating than buying a regular stock. They're valued based on the underlying commodity, similar to buying an oil miner or a copper miner. You have to understand that. Since gold and silver miners are so dependent on the price of gold and silver, you have to know why you're owning them.

You never want to own these things for short-term periods of 12 months or less. If you do that, it's more of a trade — you're just speculating on a short-term trade, and the risk increases even more. What we're trying to do is identify why we own gold and why we own these mining stocks.

For me, it really comes down to one thing: the debt bubble. I don't believe that the US, the largest economy in the world, can live on debt — can have more debt than revenue, which is what's happened this century. Debt has increased at a faster rate annually than GDP. Our debt is up to $40 trillion and we're adding $2 trillion a year. On a percentage basis, that's about 5%, but our GDP is not growing at 5%. We continue to add more debt than revenue. The reason why I own gold is it's just an inevitability unless the US can fix the debt bubble or get the economy growing faster than debt. I don't think that's possible. It's kind of a fait accompli investment.

Don Durant: Gold and Silver Price Targets (24-36 Month Horizon)

We're currently around $4,100-$4,150 gold and $65 silver.

Gold target: $7,000 is a conservative number for the next 24 to 36 months. The reason gold ripped from $2,000 in early 2024 to $5,600 in early 2026 is because of the US bond market — the fragility of the bond market, the fear of underlying issues which causes inflation and possible meltdown. Gold is a run-to asset when there are problems. When gold is ripping, it comes down to that bond market. If they don't fix this problem, and I don't think they will in the near term, $7,000 gold is a very conservative target.

Silver target: This is a story about gold. Gold goes higher and silver will follow. Central banks don't buy silver — they buy gold. Right now, central banks hold more gold in valuation than US dollars. Using the silver-to-gold ratio (silver divided into gold): today it's about 1.5%. It got to 3.5% in 2011 and 6% in 1980 (though 6% is misleading because of the Hunt Brothers). A realistic target is 2.5% to 4%. At $7,000 gold and 3%, silver would be $210. I use $200 as my target — conservative but realistic. $150 is very conservative, $200 is a good number.

Downside risk: I don't think gold can get pushed below $4,000 for any sustained period. Any time gold goes below $4,000 it will be short-lived. $3,500 is as low as I think we'll see it, and there's a good chance we don't see $3,500. The risk/reward is very asymmetric — very low downside, excellent upside. Silver upside is even more dramatic, potentially $200-$400.

Time frame: 24 to 36 months. Middle of 2028 to middle of 2029 is when I think this bull market ends. Going out more than three years, the risk of the bull market ending starts to increase.

Don Durant: Investment Pyramid for Gold and Silver Mining Stocks

The best way to own these investments is through a pyramid structure:

Base (lowest risk, largest allocation): Physical gold and silver, ETFs, mutual funds, and major producers. This is your strong foundation.

Middle (moderate risk): Mid-tier producers and quality developers.

Top (highest risk, smallest allocation): Explorers and lower-quality developers. Less allocation as you go up the pyramid.

The best risk/reward in the mining sector is a producer that is undervalued and has production growth coming. That's where you want to be overweight, because producers will participate in a big way when gold rises. The second-best is high-quality developers.

Don Durant: Checklist for Evaluating Gold and Silver Mining Developers

When evaluating a developer, look for these factors:

1. Strong project: What is the grade? How good is the recovery rate? What is the mine life? Look at the economics: NPV vs. capex ratio, AISC, IRR. We want at least a 10-bagger potential because things can go wrong and you need a lot of room for error.

2. Good location: Ideally in a proven mining district with existing infrastructure.

3. Strong management team: Track record of building and operating mines.

4. Clear path to production: Concrete timeline with milestones and financing plan.

5. Strong insiders: Management and major shareholders with significant skin in the game. Insider ownership is a key signal of conviction.

The 10-bagger rule is critical for developers: if you're not looking at at least 10x potential upside at your commodity price target, the risk doesn't justify the investment — things go wrong with development projects and you need enough upside to absorb setbacks.

Don Durant: Valuation Methodology for Future Producers

To value a future producer, I use future free cash flow at my commodity price target ($7,000 gold):

1. Estimate future annual gold production (ounces per year at full build-out)
2. Subtract expected all-in sustaining costs (AISC) to get operating free cash flow per ounce
3. Multiply by production to get total annual free cash flow
4. Apply a multiple (typically 15-20x in a bull market with strong sentiment) to get target market cap
5. Divide by shares outstanding to get target share price
6. Divide target share price by current share price to get the potential multiple

When a company's future free cash flow (at $7,000 gold) exceeds its current market cap, that's typically a 10-bagger or better — which is what you're looking for in a developer.

The multiple expands as sentiment improves: in a strong bull market with positive sentiment toward Canadian producers, multiples can reach 18-20x. In a more subdued environment, 12-15x is more realistic.

Key metric: compare the future free cash flow to the initial capex. If the free cash flow significantly exceeds the capex within the first few years of production, the project has exceptional economics.
